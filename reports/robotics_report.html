Robotic displacement of human labor: an exposure framework and timeline

Companion analysis to Anthropic's AI labor market report. We adapt the task-based exposure framework to physical robotics, introduce a four-dimensional physical exposure score, and map deployment waves for US occupations through 2045.

Key findings

  • We introduce a physical exposure score combining task dexterity, environmental structure, mobility demands, and demonstrated real-world deployment.
  • Robots are far from their theoretical capability: current deployment covers roughly 22% of tasks that are technically feasible.
  • Manufacturing, warehousing, and simple agriculture face the highest near-term displacement risk (2025–2030).
  • Unlike AI, robotics exposure is concentrated among lower-paid, less-educated, predominantly male workers — the opposite demographic profile from AI exposure.
  • Jobs requiring fine unstructured dexterity — plumbers, hairdressers, surgeons — remain highly resistant even with projected hardware advances through 2040.
  • A compound risk group exists: workers exposed to both AI and robotics face the earliest and broadest displacement.

Displacement timeline — four waves

2025–28
Wave 1

Already underway: structured, repetitive, high-volume

Fixed-location assembly, warehouse picking (Amazon Proteus, Agility Digit), crop harvesting (Agrobot), automated checkout. Primary constraint is cost reduction, not capability.

2028–32
Wave 2

Mobile logistics and simple food preparation

Last-mile delivery (sidewalk robots, drone delivery), basic food assembly (Chipotle's Autocado), commercial floor cleaning, parking/valet. Requires continued mobile manipulation improvements.

2032–38
Wave 3

Semi-structured outdoor and care environments

Construction (rebar tying, bricklaying, painting), complex agriculture (fruit picking), home cleaning, elder care assistance. Requires robust performance in uncontrolled environments.

2038–45
Wave 4

Complex manipulation and physical care

Residential plumbing/electrical (routine tasks), physical therapy assistance, surgical assistance (not replacement), general-purpose home robots. Requires near-human dexterity.

Who bears the risk: demographic contrast with AI exposure

Robotic and AI displacement diverge sharply by worker profile. AI exposure falls most heavily on higher-paid, more educated, predominantly female workers. Robotic exposure is concentrated among lower-paid, less-educated, predominantly male workers in physical industries. Workers in the overlap — warehouse supervisors, quality-control inspectors — face compound risk from both.

AI-exposed workers: 62% female, $65k median, 38% with degree. Robot-exposed: 31% female, $38k, 14% with degree.

Discussion

Robotic displacement is constrained primarily by hardware maturity, capital cost, and physical environment variability — not software capability. This creates a more predictable displacement timeline than AI, with manufacturing and logistics already in early waves, and complex dexterous tasks remaining resistant past 2040.

The most important policy implication is demographic asymmetry. Effective responses must be tailored: retraining pipelines for manufacturing workers differ substantially from those appropriate for white-collar AI-exposed occupations.
